# Evolution

A working note on how change actually happens — and how I have been working with it for years before I had words for it.

---

## Two findings, one shape

In May 2025, Jordan Douglas and colleagues at the University of Auckland published a paper called *Evolution is coupled with branching across many granularities of life.* They built a probabilistic model that estimates how much of a lineage's change happens slowly across stable populations versus suddenly at the moment a lineage splits in two, and they ran it on three things that share nothing in common: aminoacyl tRNA synthetases (the enzymes that translate the genetic code, dating to the dawn of life), the morphology of cephalopods (shells, fins, tentacles), and the Indo-European language family. In every case the slow contribution was small. In the cephalopods it was nearly zero — the model estimated that ~99% of morphological change coincided with speciation events. They prefer the term *saltative branching* to "punctuated equilibrium." The real verb of evolution is not *drift*, it is *fork*.

A few months earlier, in Science, Giacomo Gattoni and Maria Antonietta Tosches published a perspective called *Constrained roads to complex brains.* Their summary of the latest data is disconcerting. Birds and mammals both have a pallium that supports complex problem-solving, tool use, and elaborate social behavior. The pallia look surprisingly similar at the level of circuits — the wiring diagrams converge. And yet the *cells* that make up those circuits come from different developmental regions, are born at different embryonic times, and run on different genetic programs. The intelligence is convergent — built independently in two lineages — but the pieces it is built from are not. Two roads, the same destination, different stones.

Read together, the two papers do the same demolition job on the same intuition, from two different angles. Douglas et al kill smooth gradualism on the *time axis*: change does not flow, it jumps. Gattoni & Tosches kill it on the *substrate axis*: the substrate is fungible, the form is what is invariant. Different threads, same casualty.

---

## The three claims

There are three claims in the model. They are not three lessons. They are three sides of the same observation.

### 1. Change happens at forks. The dwell time loads the spring.

Stable forms barely move. The cephalopod that does not speciate looks like its grandparent. The synthetase that is not duplicated is the same enzyme it was a billion years ago. The language community that does not split keeps its words. Almost all change is concentrated at the moment a lineage forks off something new. *Drift* is rare and small; *fork* is rare and enormous.

But the dwell time between forks is not dead air. It is where the latent potential gets accumulated — the gene that will eventually be duplicated has to exist before it can be duplicated, the cuttlefish that will eventually spin off an octopus has to be a stable cuttlefish first, the worker who eventually leaves has to spend years building the kind of work that makes leaving possible. The dwell time loads the spring. The fork releases what the dwell time prepared. Iterate carefully and you load the spring; fork well and you release it. The claim is not that iteration is wasted. The claim is that iteration is preparation, and the event you are preparing for is a fork.

### 2. The destinations are constrained. The routes are many.

There are not many viable shapes for what works. Birds and mammals did not have to converge on similar pallial circuits — they did because, given the problem of building a thinking brain out of cells, there are only so many configurations that hold together. The space of viable forms has structure. It has basins and ridges and narrow passes, and the search is pulled toward the same handful of attractors regardless of where it started. *The route was never the load-bearing thing.* What is load-bearing is the destination, and the destinations were already implied by the constraints.

This is comforting in a way the saltative claim is not. If you are in the middle of a fork — leaving a job, leaving a city, leaving a framing of yourself — the destination is more constrained than it feels. There are not infinite places you might land. There are a small number of viable shapes for what you are trying to become, and the search will pull you toward them whether you can see them yet or not.

### 3. The lifting is real. Forms reach positions their substrate alone could not.

A turtle on a fence did not get there by itself. Someone put it there. This is the missing piece in any honest account of how change happens to people. The substrate of a turtle does not include fence-climbing. The substrate of a person does not include most of the positions people reach. The thing that gets you to the unlikely position is not your body, your code, or your talent — it is the network of other agents who do the lifting that your substrate alone cannot do.

This is not a soft claim. It is the same structural claim as constrained convergence, applied to the routes instead of the destinations. The routes between forks include the people who teach you, the rooms you are let into, the moments someone with more leverage than you decides to spend it on you. Tosches's pallium is built from cells in different regions by different developmental programs because the lifting is real even at the level of cells. The form is the form. The substrate is replaceable. The lifters are how the form ends up where it does.

---

## At every scale

This is not a fact about biology. Biology is just where the pattern is easiest to see, because the lineages are clean and the data go back a long way. Once you have the shape, you find it everywhere.

**Proteins.** Aminoacyl tRNA synthetases do not slowly slide into new functions. New function tracks gene duplication — a fork in the genome. After duplication one copy is free to drift, and the new function comes in jumps. Without the duplication, the protein stays what it was for a billion years.

**Morphology.** Ninety-nine percent. That is the figure for cephalopods. The cephalopod body does not gradually become an octopus. It becomes one the moment a lineage splits off and becomes its own thing.

**Brains.** Bird and mammal pallia, independently arrived at, built from different neurons in different developmental regions, converge on the same circuit. There are not many ways to build a thinking thing, and the routes that get there look nothing like each other.

**Languages.** Indo-European splits and re-splits. The dialect that becomes a separate language is the dialect that crosses the mountains and gets cut off. Slow drift inside a stable speech community produces almost nothing. Sociopolitical division produces almost everything.

**Ideas.** A worldview does not slowly improve in place. It cracks. Some part of you forks off, takes a few of the old beliefs, drops the rest, and becomes a person who thinks differently. The interval between forks looks like stability. It is dwell time, and it is loading the next spring.

**Products.** The next version of a product is not the previous version with more features. The products that matter are the ones that fork off some piece of an existing thing and become their own organism with their own pressures.

**AI.** The discrete-math machinery originally built to *model* the world is now being used to *navigate* it. People who use the new substrate well are evolving on a micro scale. The brain is being rewired by the tool it is coupled to. This is the same shape as the others; it is not special, it is just current.

**People.** You do not become a different person by gradually trying harder at being the current one. You become a different person by branching: leaving a job, leaving a city, leaving a relationship, leaving a framing of yourself that no longer holds. The years between branches are dwell time. The transformation you keep waiting for inside the current form is not coming. It happens at the fork, and the dwell time is what makes the fork productive.

---

## What this asks of you

If you accept the picture, four things follow.

*Name the dwell state honestly.* You cannot fork from a form you refuse to see clearly. Most people stay inside a stable form because they cannot bring themselves to say what the form actually is. Call it. Acknowledge where you are. The acknowledgment is the prerequisite for the move.

*Build the parallel substrate during dwell time.* Do not wait for the fork to start preparing. The dwell time is the time. Whatever the next form needs — a craft, a network, a body of work, a piece of capital, a way of thinking — assemble it now. The fork will be the moment the substrate you have been quietly building becomes the new form.

*Trust the landing.* The destinations are constrained. There are not infinite places you might end up; there is a small number of viable shapes, and the constraints will pull you toward them. Branching feels like jumping into nothing. It is not. It is jumping into a basin you cannot see yet but which is there.

*Acknowledge the lifters.* The turtle did not get there alone. Neither did you. The form that ends up in the unlikely position is the form that was lifted there by people with more leverage than the form alone had. Name them. Pay them back. Become a lifter for someone whose substrate cannot reach the position they belong in.

---

## Closing

Models like this rarely arrive as discoveries. They arrive as language for things you have already been doing — a name for a shape your decisions already had, a vocabulary for a habit that has been running unsupervised for years. The first time I read these two papers, what I noticed was not new information. It was that I had been running this model for as long as I had been making my own decisions, and I had not had words for it. I had spent years quietly assembling parallel substrates inside forms I was preparing to leave. I had been telling people, in clumsier language, that you have to acknowledge where you are before you can move from it. I had been keeping track of the people who had lifted me into rooms my own work alone could not have reached. I did not have biology to point at. I had a habit that the biology eventually confirmed.

That is the point. The model is not borrowed from biology. Biology is just the cleanest place to see it. The pattern is structural, and it is the same pattern whether the search is being run by selection on tRNA enzymes, by speciation in cephalopods, by independent developmental programs converging on a thinking circuit, by drift across mountain ranges, or by you in the dwell time between your last fork and your next one.

The form has limits, and inside the limits there is enough room. The world is not a smooth landscape. It is a discrete topology of viable forms separated by gaps, connected by rare branching events, traveled with the help of agents who lift the form past what its substrate alone could reach. Most of the time you are loading the spring. Some of the time you are at the fork.

*Name the dwell state. Then move.*
