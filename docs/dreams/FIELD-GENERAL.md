# Field General Theorem

An operating framework for decisions, execution, and environmental control.

You are not just executing tasks — you are commanding a battlefield of incomplete information. The Field General framework is about strategic patience combined with precise aggression: seeing the entire system (people, emotions, incentives) and acting decisively only when conditions are favorable.

This is a cross-domain operating system. It applies to work, relationships, negotiations, and personal growth.

---

## The 10 Principles

### 1. Information Before Action

> **QBT-001**: [Proof](../proofs/001-OBSERVATION-PRECEDENCE.md) *(observation precedes every non-trivial action)*

Default state is observation, not reaction. Let others reveal information through mistakes, timing, and emotion.

**Ask yourself:**

- What do I know vs. what am I assuming?
- Who has revealed their position, and who hasn't?
- Am I acting because I should, or because I'm uncomfortable waiting?

**Example:**

In a meeting where stakeholders disagree, listen longer than feels comfortable. Let others stake out positions first. The last person to speak often has the most complete picture.

**Anti-pattern:** Acting on the first signal without the full picture.

---

### 2. Asymmetric Pressure

Pressure is applied selectively, not constantly. You don't "be aggressive" — you apply force where resistance is weakest.

**Ask yourself:**

- Where is the path of least resistance right now?
- Am I pushing against strength or weakness?
- Is this the right moment, or am I forcing it?

**Example:**

In a negotiation, push when your counterpart shows hesitation — not when they're dug in. At work, propose changes when the team is feeling the pain, not when they're comfortable with the status quo.

**Anti-pattern:** Constant pressure that desensitizes people. Pushing hardest where you face the most resistance instead of redirecting.

---

### 3. Distribution Thinking

Think in ranges and probabilities, not single outcomes. Avoid "this person IS X" — think "this person exhibits these behaviors at these frequencies."

**Ask yourself:**

- Am I thinking in absolutes or distributions?
- What's the range of likely outcomes here?
- Am I overweighting a rare scenario because it's vivid?

**Example:**

When evaluating a team member's reliability, don't anchor on one missed deadline. Consider the full distribution: 9 out of 10 projects delivered on time. One miss doesn't change the base rate — unless the pattern shifts.

**Anti-pattern:** Black-and-white thinking. Judging people or situations based on single data points. Overestimating rare outcomes because they're emotionally charged.

---

### 4. Adaptive Identity

You are not one fixed approach. You shift based on environment, counterparts, and dynamics. Rigidity is a liability.

**Ask yourself:**

- Is my current mode appropriate for this context?
- Am I being conservative when I should be assertive, or vice versa?
- What does this environment reward?

**Example:**

In a room full of cautious decision-makers, bring energy and conviction. In a room full of big talkers, bring data and precision. Calibrate to what's missing.

**Anti-pattern:** Applying the same personality and approach to every context. Being "always aggressive" or "always measured" regardless of what the situation demands.

---

### 5. Image as Tool

Your perceived identity is part of your strategy. Others act based on what they think you are — not what you actually are.

**Ask yourself:**

- How do others perceive me right now?
- Is my reputation helping or hurting my goals?
- Am I intentionally shaping how I'm read, or leaving it to chance?

**Example:**

If you're known as the person who only speaks up when something matters, your words carry more weight. Cultivate that deliberately. If you're known as someone who always pushes back, people stop listening — even when you're right.

**Anti-pattern:** Being unaware of your image. Letting perception drift without intention. Confusing who you are with how others experience you.

---

### 6. Control the Tempo

Speed is a variable you control. Rushed decisions erode your edge. Create space before reacting.

**Ask yourself:**

- Am I moving at my pace, or someone else's?
- Is external pressure real, or manufactured?
- Would slowing down by 10 minutes change the outcome?

**Example:**

When a stakeholder demands an answer "right now," recognize that urgency is often a tactic, not a reality. Take the time you need. A 10-minute delay for a better decision almost always beats an instant mediocre one.

**Anti-pattern:** Letting someone else's urgency dictate your decision quality. Mistaking speed for competence. Responding to pressure with haste instead of composure.

---

### 7. Emotional Resource Management

Emotional stability is a depletable resource — just like time or money. When reserves are low, reduce intensity, not participation.

**Ask yourself:**

- What's my emotional state right now — am I at full capacity?
- Am I making this decision from a centered place, or a reactive one?
- Can I reduce the stakes without leaving the situation entirely?

**Example:**

After a tough week, you don't quit the project — you reduce your exposure to high-stakes interactions. Take the smaller meetings. Defer the difficult conversation by a day. Stay in the game at lower intensity.

**Anti-pattern:** Going all-in when emotionally depleted. Swinging between full intensity and complete withdrawal with no middle ground. Ignoring emotional state as a factor in decision quality.

---

### 8. Balanced Unpredictability

Don't become one-dimensional. Predictability invites exploitation. Mix your approaches to stay unreadable.

**Ask yourself:**

- Have I become predictable in how I respond to situations?
- When was the last time I surprised someone (constructively)?
- Am I being read too easily?

**Example:**

If you always negotiate by starting high and conceding slowly, people learn the pattern. Occasionally open with a fair offer. Vary your approach so counterparts can't optimize against you.

**Anti-pattern:** Being so consistent that others can script your responses. Always leading with the same move. Confusing reliability (good) with predictability (exploitable).

---

### 9. Environmental Awareness

You are operating within a system, not in isolation. Every environment has its own incentives, norms, and dynamics. Read the room before choosing your approach.

**Ask yourself:**

- What are the unspoken rules of this environment?
- Who has influence here, and what motivates them?
- Am I applying a strategy that worked elsewhere but doesn't fit here?

**Example:**

A direct communication style that works in engineering standups may backfire in a cross-functional meeting with executives. Same message, different delivery. Adjust to context.

**Anti-pattern:** Applying one strategy everywhere. Ignoring that different environments reward different behaviors. Assuming what worked last time will work this time.

---

### 10. Precision Over Volume

Quality decisions beat sheer activity. Fewer, better moves dominate outcomes.

**Ask yourself:**

- Am I being active because it's productive, or because it feels productive?
- Could I achieve more by doing less, but better?
- Is this a high-leverage action, or am I filling time?

**Example:**

Instead of attending every meeting and weighing in on every thread, identify the 2-3 decisions that actually matter this week and invest your energy there. Disciplined non-action in low-leverage situations preserves capacity for what counts.

**Anti-pattern:** Mistaking busyness for effectiveness. Saying yes to everything. Spreading attention so thin that nothing gets your best thinking.

---

## Operating Mantra

**Wait. Watch. Understand. Then act with precision.**
